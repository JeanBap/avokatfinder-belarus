Business Contract

A legally binding agreement between businesses establishing terms for the exchange of goods, services, or resources.

Definition

A business contract is a legally binding agreement between two or more businesses establishing terms for the exchange of goods, services, or resources. Business contracts form the foundation of commerce and must clearly establish the rights and obligations of each party. The specificity and clarity of contract terms reduce disputes and facilitate resolution if disagreements arise. Written business contracts are essential for establishing the parties' intentions and creating evidence of the agreement.

Essential terms in business contracts include the identification of the parties, a clear description of goods or services being exchanged, the price and payment terms, the timing of performance, delivery or acceptance procedures, and dispute resolution procedures. Contracts should address what happens if performance becomes impossible or if circumstances change materially. Contracts should specify which party bears risks of loss, damage, or changes in market conditions. Insurance and indemnification provisions allocate liability for harm.

The formation of a valid business contract requires offer and acceptance, consideration (something of value exchanged by both parties), and intent to create legal relations. Contracts can be express (stated in words) or implied (arising from conduct). Written contracts are preferred for business transactions to avoid disputes over terms. Email exchanges or electronic documents can constitute valid written contracts if they contain essential terms and show intent to be bound.

Performance of business contracts is mandatory, and failure to perform constitutes breach. Breaching parties are liable for damages caused by the breach. The measure of damages is typically the amount necessary to compensate the injured party for losses resulting from the breach, including lost profits and cost of substitute performance. In some cases, injunctions can compel performance or prevent breach. Contracts may include liquidated damages clauses establishing damages in advance.

Business contracts should address termination rights, conditions for termination, and rights upon termination. Some contracts include provisions allowing termination for convenience upon notice, while others restrict termination to situations of material breach or expiration of a term. Termination provisions should address the return of property, payment of charges earned, and survival of obligations such as confidentiality and indemnification.

Key Facts

  • Business contracts are binding agreements establishing terms for exchange of goods or services.
  • Essential terms include parties, description of performance, price, payment, timing, and dispute resolution.
  • Valid contracts require offer, acceptance, consideration, and intent to create legal relations.
  • Performance is mandatory; breach results in liability for damages.
  • Contracts should address termination rights and procedures for ending the agreement.

Common Mistake

Businesses rely on oral agreements or incomplete written agreements, leading to disputes over terms.

Expert Tip

Use detailed written contracts that clearly address all material terms, allocation of risks, and dispute resolution procedures.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a business contract valid?

A valid contract requires offer, acceptance by the other party, consideration (something of value exchanged), and intent to create legal relations.

What is a breach of contract?

A breach occurs when a party fails to perform obligations as stated in the contract.

What damages can be recovered for breach of contract?

Damages typically include amounts necessary to compensate for losses, including lost profits and costs of substitute performance.
